Universal Design Exhibit Manual

Administration and Use

0 criteria

The Universal Design Exhibit Checklist is an observation-based checklist intended to support reflection on how museum exhibits support broad participation. The tool is designed to document strengths and areas for growth related to Universal Design principles and is not intended to produce a numerical score or determine compliance. The tool is completed through live observation of an exhibit during typical use. Exhibits may be observed during free play, facilitated activities, or a combination of both. Observations may be completed in a single session or across multiple visits, depending on the goals of the assessment. Each section of the tool is organized by a Universal Design principle and includes a series of observable criteria. Reviewers should read each criterion and consider whether the exhibit meets the item based on what is observed during use.

For each criterion, a checkbox is provided. If the exhibit clearly meets the item, the checkbox may be marked to indicate a “yes” response. When a criterion is more complex, inconsistently observed, or dependent on context, reviewers may instead use the optional rating scale to indicate whether the item is Present, Partially Present, Not Present, or Not Applicable. The rating scale is optional and does not need to be used for every item.

Space is provided for comments throughout the tool. Reviewers are encouraged to use this space to record brief observations, examples, or contextual factors that influenced their responses. Comments may include notes about how children or caregivers interacted with the exhibit, environmental factors such as crowding or noise, or specific barriers or supports observed.

There are no right or wrong responses. Reviewers should use their professional judgment and focus on observed use rather than intended design. Not all criteria will apply equally to every exhibit, and some items may be marked as Not Applicable based on the exhibit’s purpose. The results from the tool should be interpreted collectively to identify patterns, strengths, and opportunities for enhancing universally designed play experiences. Individual items are not intended to stand alone, and the tool should be used as a guide for discussion, reflection, and planning rather than evaluation or scoring.

Universal Design Exhibit Manual

Principle 1: Equitable Use

4 criteria

This section focuses on whether children with diverse abilities can participate in the exhibit without being separated, singled out, or required to use alternative versions of play experiences. Reviewers should consider whether the core experience of the exhibit is shared by all users, even when different modes of use or engagement are present.

01 The exhibit includes opportunities for physically challenging play (e.g., climbing, crawling, stairs) without making these the only means of engaging with the core experience.

This item examines whether physical challenge is offered in ways that support development while remaining usable by a wide range of users. Reviewers should observe whether climbing, crawling, stairs, or similar challenges are present and whether these elements are balanced with alternative ways to engage in the exhibit’s primary play or learning goal. The intent is not to remove challenge, but to ensure that physically demanding elements are not the sole gateway to participation.

02 Children using wheeled mobility aids (e.g., wheelchairs, walkers) can approach, reach, and interact with the same core play features as peers.

This item focuses on functional use rather than the presence of specific devices. Reviewers should observe whether children using wheeled mobility aids can independently approach the exhibit, position themselves, and interact with the main play components. Engagement should involve the same core features used by peers, not secondary or separate elements. Assistance from caregivers should not be required for basic use.

03 No separate or “special” version of the activity is required to participate.

This item examines whether participation is integrated rather than segregated. Reviewers should look for design features that allow children with different abilities to engage together in the same space and activity. The item is met when usable by a wide range of users features are embedded within the exhibit itself rather than offered as separate adaptations.

04 The exhibit does not rely on a single sensory modality (e.g., only visual or only auditory).

This item addresses whether use to play depends on one dominant sensory channel. Reviewers should consider whether children can engage with the exhibit through more than one sensory pathway. Reliance on a single modality may limit participation for children with sensory differences or disabilities.

Principle 2: Flexibility in Use

5 criteria

This supports variation in how children can play in an environment. The principle in general includes guidelines such as choice in methods of use, accommodating use for right- and left-hand dominant users, and having adaptability to users’ pace (Connell, 1997). Reviewers should consider differences in pace, intensity, developmental level, and whether the exhibit allows children to make choices about how they can participate.

01 The exhibit supports multiple ways to engage (e.g. watch, touch, move, pretend, manipulate).

This item examines whether children can engage with the exhibit in more than one manner. Reviewers should look for opportunities to observe, explore physically, manipulate objects, engage in imaginative play, or interact socially. The item is met when engagement limited to a single action or behavior. Tools in the exhibit must enable use in a variety of ways. For example, magnetic tiles may be used in a variety of ways; however, rigid toys like electronic dolls that only complete one action with a button are not flexible in use. For exhibits that have excessive tools and toys in the exhibit, the majority should reflect open-ended use. Not all must be flexible to receive a yes; it is the reviewer’s discretion to interpret if the exhibit reflects flexible use. The following are some questions that can be asked when evaluating this criterion. Even if some toys in the exhibit are not flexible, does the exhibit provide reflect flexibility as a whole? Are there a variety of ways to engage with the exhibit?

02 Children can choose how long and how intensely to engage with the activity.

This criterion is easily observed when it comes to the length of engagement. However, the intensity of engagement is nuanced and requires more interpretation. Reviewers should observe whether children are able to enter and exit play freely, adjust their pace, or vary their level of effort without disrupting the activity or relying on adult control. Exhibits that force prolonged engagement or fixed pacing may limit flexibility. Reviewers should consider if participants that input different levels of intensity receive equal or similar opportunities of play.

03 The exhibit supports independent engagement and cooperative play without requiring adult mediation.

This item examines whether the design allows children to engage alone or with others naturally. Reviewers should look for features that support parallel play, shared use, or collaboration without requiring adults to explain rules, manage turn-taking, or structure interaction. Adult involvement may enhance play but should not be necessary for basic engagement.

04 Materials or components can be used in more than one way (not a single “right” outcome).

This item addresses openness and creativity in play. Reviewers should consider whether materials allow for exploration, experimentation, or multiple outcomes rather than one correct way to use them. Exhibits that support open-ended use tend to accommodate a wider range of abilities and play preferences.

05 The exhibit can be used by children at different developmental stages and abilities without modification.

This item examines whether children of varying ages or developmental levels can engage meaningfully with the same exhibit. Reviewers should observe whether the exhibit naturally supports different skill levels through scaling, variation, or open-ended design, rather than requiring changes or adaptations to be usable.

Principle 3: Simple and Intuitive Use

4 criteria

This principle focuses on whether children can understand how to engage with the exhibit without relying on verbal instruction, reading, or adult explanation. Reviewers should consider whether the design itself communicates how play begins and unfolds.

01 The purpose of the exhibit is clear without adult explanation.

This item examines whether children can understand what the exhibit is for by observing its layout, materials, and interactive elements. Reviewers should consider whether children are able to initiate play independently or whether repeated adult direction is required to explain the exhibit’s purpose.

02 The exhibit’s design makes it clear where and how children can begin interacting without adult instruction.

This item focuses on entry into play. Reviewers should look for obvious starting points, inviting objects, or interactive elements that naturally draw children in. Clear affordances, such as objects meant to be touched, spaces meant to be entered, or elements that respond immediately to interaction, support intuitive use.

03 Key aspects of play and learning are usable without reading, or supported through visuals, symbols, or modeling when text is used intentionally.

This item examines whether reading is a gatekeeper to participation. Reviewers should consider whether children can engage meaningfully through observation, symbols, or physical interaction. When text is present, it should be supplemented with visuals, symbols, or modeling that support understanding across literacy levels.

04 Children can successfully engage through trial and error.

This item focuses on whether the exhibit supports exploration without fear of failure. Reviewers should observe whether children can experiment, make mistakes, and adjust their actions without negative consequences or frustration. Exhibits that allow learning through doing support intuitive engagement.

Principle 4: Perceptible Information

7 criteria

This principle focuses on how information within an exhibit is communicated and whether children and caregivers can perceive key information through more than one sensory channel. Reviewers should consider how information is conveyed during active play, not only through written instructions or signage. Information may include cues about how to begin, how to interact, how the exhibit responds to interaction, or how play progresses.

01 Key information is available through more than one modality (visual, tactile, auditory).

This item examines whether essential information needed to engage with the exhibit is communicated using more than one sensory modality. Reviewers should look for information that can be perceived visually, through sound, or through touch. Examples may include visual indicators paired with physical interaction, auditory feedback paired with visual change, or tactile elements that communicate meaning through texture, movement, or resistance. The exhibit does not need to include all sensory modalities, but use to key information should not rely on only one.

02 Visual information is clearly distinguishable from its background and visible across lighting conditions, heights, and viewing distances within the exhibit.

This item addresses the usability of visual information within the exhibit environment. Reviewers should consider visual elements such as signage, symbols, icons, labels, color-coded features, wall graphics, or visual cues that guide interaction. Visual information should stand out from its background through contrast, size, spacing, or placement. Reviewers should observe whether visual information remains visible under typical museum lighting conditions and whether it can be perceived from child-height, seated, and standing positions. Visual information that is only visible from a single vantage point or requires close proximity may limit usability.

This criterion examines whether visual information within the exhibit can be easily perceived by a wide range of users, including children, caregivers, individuals with low vision, and those viewing the exhibit from different physical positions. Visual information includes written instructions, labels, icons, arrows, diagrams, color-coded indicators, and wall graphics that help explain how to use the exhibit or understand what is happening.

When evaluating this item, reviewers should consider the following:

Contrast: Text or symbols should clearly stand out from their background. For example, dark text on a light background or light text on a dark background is generally easier to read than similar colors placed together (e.g., blue text on green). Information should not blend into patterned walls or decorative graphics.

Size and readability: Text and symbols should be large enough to be read from a typical viewing distance without squinting or stepping unusually close. Reviewers can informally test this by standing where a child or caregiver would naturally stand and asking whether the text or symbol can be read comfortably.

Clarity and spacing: Letters, numbers, and symbols should not be crowded. Adequate spacing between lines and elements improves readability.

Height and position: Information should be visible from child-height and seated positions, not only at adult eye level. Reviewers should briefly crouch or sit (if feasible) to assess whether key information remains visible.

Lighting and glare: Information should remain readable under typical museum lighting. Reviewers should check whether glare from overhead lights, reflections on glossy surfaces, or shadows make text difficult to read.

Dependence on proximity: Visual information should not require users to move to a single exact position to understand it. If information is only legible when standing directly in front of a panel at very close range, use may be limited.

This criterion is met when most users can quickly and comfortably perceive important visual information without strain, repositioning, or adult assistance. It is not necessary for all visual information to be perfectly optimized, but key information needed to engage with the exhibit should be usable to a broad range of users.

03 Audio elements are clear and not overwhelming or startling.

This item focuses on the quality and appropriateness of auditory information. Reviewers should observe whether sounds used in the exhibit are clear, predictable, and supportive of engagement. Audio elements should not be excessively loud, sudden, or distressing, particularly when multiple users are present. Sound should enhance understanding or engagement rather than distract from play or create sensory overload.

04 Important cues (e.g., start/stop, transitions) are communicated through more than one sensory modality.

This item examines how key cues within the exhibit are conveyed. Reviewers should look for cues that signal when interaction begins, changes, or ends, or that indicate how play progresses. These cues may be communicated visually, through touch or movement, through sound, or through a combination of sensory channels. The item is met when important cues can be perceived without relying on a single sensory modality.

05 Signage or symbols are age-appropriate and easily interpreted.

This item refers to written, symbolic, or graphic information used to support understanding or guide interaction. Reviewers should consider whether signage uses developmentally appropriate language, symbols, or imagery and whether it can be interpreted quickly without adult explanation. This includes pictorial cues, icons, or simple visual prompts intended to support engagement rather than detailed instructional text.

06 Tactile features (e.g., textures, raised elements, manipulatives) provide meaningful information or feedback during play.

This item focuses on whether tactile elements convey information rather than serving only as decorative features. Reviewers should look for textures, shapes, resistance, raised elements, or manipulatives that provide feedback, guide interaction, or help users understand how the exhibit responds to touch. Tactile features should support engagement by communicating information through physical interaction.

07 The exhibit provides at least one non-visual way to perceive play-related information (e.g., through touch or movement).

This item examines whether children can use information through means other than vision. Reviewers should observe whether touch, movement, resistance, or physical interaction communicates information that supports engagement. Examples may include feeling changes in position, resistance, vibration, or motion that help users understand how the exhibit functions during play.

Principle 5: Tolerance for Error

5 criteria

This principle focuses on whether an exhibit allows children to experiment, make mistakes, and try again without unnecessary consequences such as injury, repeated failure, or exclusion from play. In a children’s museum, tolerance for error does not mean eliminating challenge. Developmentally meaningful play often includes risk, effort, uncertainty, and problem-solving. Reviewers should therefore consider whether the exhibit offers appropriate challenge while still providing safeguards, design features, and feedback that support safe exploration and sustained engagement.

When using this section, reviewers should attend to both physical and participation-related “errors.” Physical errors may include slips, pinches, collisions, or misuse of components that create hazards. Participation-related errors may include confusing steps, unclear expectations, or fragile interactions that cause the activity to stop working when used in a typical, child-led way.

01 Children can explore freely without risk of injury from misuse.

This item examines whether typical child exploration, including non-linear or “unexpected” use, creates hazards. Reviewers should observe whether children can touch, push, pull, climb, build, take apart, or move through the space in ways that are common in play without encountering unsafe outcomes. Consider whether there are sharp edges, pinch points, unstable structures, tripping hazards, or components that can be pulled down or thrown in ways that could cause harm. This item is met when the exhibit is designed so that common play behaviors do not result in injury, while still allowing children to engage in physically active and challenging play.

02 There is no single failure point that stops engagement.

This item focuses on whether engagement depends on one specific step, object, or correct sequence. Reviewers should look for whether play can continue even if a child skips a step, uses a component differently than intended, or interacts in a non-sequential manner. Exhibits with a single “right” input that must occur for the experience to function may limit participation for children who explore differently. This item is met when the exhibit remains usable and engaging even when play is varied, imperfect, or non-linear.

03 Mistakes lead to learning opportunities rather than frustration.

This item examines how the exhibit responds when children try something that does not work. Reviewers should observe whether children can adjust their approach, try again, or receive information that helps them re-engage. “Learning opportunities” may include clear feedback, visible options for trying a different strategy, or multiple pathways to engagement. The item is met when the exhibit supports persistence and problem-solving rather than repeatedly signaling failure or ending the play experience.

04 The exhibit is durable and forgiving of rough or unexpected use.

This item focuses on whether the exhibit is designed to withstand typical child play behaviors, including high energy, repeated use, and trial-and-error interaction. Reviewers should consider whether components are sturdy, whether materials tolerate repetitive manipulation, and whether play elements remain functional even when used in ways that are not perfectly precise. An exhibit does not need to be indestructible, but it should not require careful, delicate interaction to participate. This item is met when the exhibit supports the reality of children’s play without frequent breakage, dislodging, or “do not touch” conditions.

05 Clear boundaries exist where safety is a concern (e.g., edges, moving parts).

This item examines whether hazards are anticipated and made understandable through design. Reviewers should look for whether areas that could present risk are clearly identifiable through physical barriers, visual contrast, tactile cues, spacing, or other signals that help children and caregivers recognize boundaries. Boundaries might include guardrails, buffers around moving parts, clear separation between active zones and walkways, or design cues that guide where bodies and hands should or should not go. The item is met when risk areas are clearly communicated and managed without relying solely on rules or adult enforcement.

Principle 6: Low Physical Effort

5 criteria

This principle focuses on whether physical effort within the exhibit is intentional, meaningful, and adjustable. In the context of children’s museum play, low physical effort does not mean minimal effort. Developmentally appropriate play often involves strength, endurance, balance, and coordination. Reviewers should therefore consider whether physical demands are aligned with the exhibit’s purpose and whether children can modulate their effort rather than being forced into sustained or excessive exertion to participate.

Reviewers should attend to how effort is distributed across the exhibit, whether multiple pathways for engagement exist, and whether children are able to pause, shift roles, or re-engage without disengaging entirely.

01 Physical effort required is intentional and aligned with the play or learning goal.

This item examines whether physical demands are purposeful rather than incidental. Reviewers should consider whether pushing, pulling, lifting, climbing, or reaching supports the intended play experience or learning outcome. Effort should feel meaningful within the context of play, rather than serving as an unintended barrier to engagement.

02 Children can modulate their level of physical effort (e.g., pace, duration, intensity).

This item focuses on whether children have control over how much effort they exert. Reviewers should observe whether children can slow down, speed up, take breaks, or vary their intensity without being excluded from the activity. Exhibits that require continuous effort without opportunities for adjustment may limit participation for some users.

03 Physically demanding elements are optional or paired with lower-effort ways to engage.

This item examines whether challenge is balanced with choice. Reviewers should look for alternative ways to participate that do not require the same level of strength, endurance, or coordination. This may include observational roles, alternative interaction points, or components that allow engagement without sustained physical exertion. The item is met when physically demanding elements enhance play but do not function as gatekeepers.

04 Controls or manipulatives do not require excessive strength beyond the intended challenge.

This item focuses on fine and gross motor demands related to interaction. Reviewers should consider whether handles, levers, buttons, or movable components can be operated using a range of strength levels. Resistance may be intentional, but it should not exceed what is necessary for the play goal or require repeated force that leads to rapid fatigue.

05 Opportunities to pause, rest, or observe are naturally available within the play space.

This item examines whether children can disengage temporarily without leaving the exhibit entirely. Reviewers should look for places to sit, lean, watch others, or interact in lower-effort ways. Opportunities for rest support self-regulation and allow children to re-engage when ready, particularly during physically active play.

Principle 7: Size and Space for Approach and Use

5 criteria

This principle focuses on whether the physical layout of the exhibit supports use, movement, and interaction for children and caregivers with diverse body sizes, mobility patterns, and participation needs. Reviewers should consider not only whether space is technically available, but whether it is usable during real-world conditions such as crowding, shared play, and caregiver involvement.

01 Clear pathways allow children and caregivers to approach without congestion.

This item examines whether pathways leading to and around the exhibit allow for movement without bottlenecks. Reviewers should observe whether children and caregivers can approach, circulate, and exit the space without blocking others or being forced to wait. Consider how strollers, mobility aids, and groups affect flow during typical use.

02 A clear turning space (approximately 60 inches in diameter or equivalent) is available to allow children using wheeled mobility aids to turn or reposition independently.

This item focuses on functional maneuverability. Reviewers should observe whether there is sufficient open floor space at key interaction points for turning or repositioning without assistance. Turning space should be available where children are expected to stop and engage, not only at the perimeter of the exhibit.

03 Interactive components are within reachable heights for a range of users.

This item examines whether children of different heights and body positions can reach and use interactive elements. Reviewers should consider whether components can be accessed from seated, standing, and child-height positions. Items that require sustained reaching or precise positioning may limit participation.

04 Multiple children can engage without crowding.

This item focuses on whether the exhibit supports shared use. Reviewers should observe whether multiple children can interact simultaneously without interfering with one another’s movement or use. Crowding that consistently limits participation may indicate insufficient space or too few interaction points.

05 The space supports adult participation in play.

This item examines whether caregivers can participate alongside children without obstructing use or flow. Reviewers should consider whether adults can kneel, sit, stand, or move with children comfortably and whether their presence supports rather than disrupts engagement. Adult participation is particularly important for younger children and children who benefit from shared play or assistance.
